# Multiple Monsters

## Multiple Attack System

Creatures with multiple Attacks can engage multiple opponents simultaneously. The system determines how many attacks can be made and what happens when a creature has more attacks than available targets.

---

## Standard Rules

A creature with more than one Attack can simultaneously attack multiple enemies:

- They roll separately for each attack.
- They are only outnumbered if the number of enemies they face is **more than** the number of Attacks they have.
- If a creature with three Attacks faces three Heroes, all three can be attacked.
- If the same creature faces a single Hero, only one attack may be made.

**Example**: A Giant with three Attacks fighting three Heroes can attack all three Heroes, rolling separately for each attack.

**Example**: If the same Giant faces only one Hero, they can only make one attack against that Hero.

## Alternative Rule: Unused Attack Bonuses

**Source:** CB77021 - Combat Companion, Page 23 [Page 0023](../../../CB77021_Combat_Companion_Pages/page_0023.png)

**Note:** This is an alternative rule. In EPIC, this rule is in full effect.

In standard Advanced Fighting Fantasy rules, a creature with more than one Attack can simultaneously attack multiple enemies, rolling separately for each. A creature is only outnumbered if the number of enemies they face is more than the number of Attacks they have.

The **Unused Attack Bonuses** rule makes creatures with multiple attacks more dangerous when they cannot use all of their attacks, as unused attacks translate into combat bonuses.

### Unused Attack Bonuses

Every Attack that a monster is not able to use (because they do not have enough opponents to attack) translates into a bonus of **+2 to an attack they do make**.

**Example**: A Giant with three Attacks fights two Heroes. The Giant has one "unused" attack and thus can apply a **+2 bonus** to one of the combat rolls they make.

**Example**: If the Giant defeats one of the Heroes and is now fighting only one, they would have two "unused" attacks and would have a bonus of **+4** against that final Hero.

### Restrictions

- The **+2 bonus may not be split** between multiple attacks.
- The bonus **must be assigned at the beginning of the round**.
- A monster **may not voluntarily keep attacks unused** if they have enough opponents.

**Example**: If a Giant with three Attacks is fighting three Heroes, they cannot choose to make only two attacks in order to get a +2 bonus from the unused attack. The Giant **MUST attack all three Heroes**.

---

## Using Multiple Monsters in Play

- When a creature with multiple Attacks faces fewer opponents than their Attack value, calculate unused attacks.
- Each unused attack provides a +2 bonus to one attack roll.
- The bonus must be assigned at the beginning of the combat round.
- The bonus cannot be split between multiple attacks.
- Creatures cannot voluntarily hold back attacks if they have enough opponents to use all attacks.

### Multiple Monsters in Combat

**Outnumbering:** A creature is only outnumbered if the number of enemies they face is **more than** the number of Attacks they have. A creature with three Attacks facing three Heroes is not outnumbered.

**Unused Attacks:** When a creature has more Attacks than opponents, unused Attacks become combat bonuses. A creature with three Attacks facing one Hero gets +4 to their single attack (two unused attacks Ã— +2 each).

**Tactical Implications:** This rule makes powerful multi-attack creatures significantly more dangerous when facing fewer opponents, encouraging Heroes to work together and maintain numerical advantages in combat.

**Multiple Opponents:** When a creature faces multiple opponents equal to or less than their Attack value, they can attack all opponents, rolling separately for each attack.
